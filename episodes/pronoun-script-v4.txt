## SECTION 1: Direct Object Pronouns

Now let's start with direct object pronouns. These little words are essential in making your Italian sound more fluent and natural.

[PAUSE 2s]

Direct object pronouns in Italian replace the direct object in a sentence. A direct object is the noun or pronoun that receives the action of the verb directly, without any preposition like "a" or "to" in English. For example, in the sentence "I see the book," "the book" is the direct object because it receives the action of "see." In Italian, we could replace "the book" with a direct object pronoun.

[PAUSE 3s]

### The Forms of Direct Object Pronouns

Here are the direct object pronouns in Italian:

[IT:mi] - me
[IT:ti] - you, in the informal singular
[IT:lo] - him, or it when referring to something masculine
[IT:la] - her, or it when referring to something feminine
[IT:ci] - us
[IT:vi] - you all, in the informal plural
[IT:li] - them, when referring to masculine nouns
[IT:le] - them, when referring to feminine nouns

[PAUSE 3s]

Let me repeat those slowly: [IT:mi]... [IT:ti]... [IT:lo]... [IT:la]... [IT:ci]... [IT:vi]... [IT:li]... [IT:le].

[PAUSE 4s]

### Position Rules for Direct Object Pronouns

In Italian, direct object pronouns usually precede the conjugated verb. For instance:

[IT:Ti vedo ogni giorno] - I see you every day.

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT:La chiamo domani] - I call her tomorrow.

[PAUSE 3s]

However, when an infinitive verb is involved, the pronoun can be attached to the end of the infinitive:

[IT:Voglio vederlo] - I want to see him, or I want to see it when it's masculine.

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT:Devo chiamarla] - I must call her, or I must call it when it's feminine.

[PAUSE 4s]

### Elision with [IT:lo] and [IT:la]

When [IT:lo] or [IT:la] comes before a verb that starts with a vowel, they become [IT:l'] for ease of pronunciation. Note that this does not happen with [IT:li] or [IT:le].

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT:L'ascolto] - I listen to it, him, or her.

[IT:L'ho comprato] - I bought it, when referring to something masculine.

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT:L'ho comprata] - I bought it, when referring to something feminine. Notice how the ending changes to agree.

### Agreement in Passato Prossimo

A critical aspect of using direct object pronouns in Italian is agreement in passato prossimo. When the direct object pronoun precedes a compound tense like passato prossimo, the past participle must agree in gender and number with the pronoun.

## SECTION 2: Indirect Object Pronouns

Now let's move on to indirect object pronouns. These pronouns answer the question "to whom?" or "for whom?" and are used to replace indirect objects in sentences. In Italian, indirect objects are often introduced by the preposition [IT:a].

[PAUSE 3s]

### What Indirect Pronouns Replace

Indirect object pronouns in Italian replace phrases that include "to whom" or "for whom." For instance, in the sentence "I give the book to Maria," "to Maria" is the indirect object. In Italian, you would use an indirect pronoun to replace "Maria" and simplify the sentence. So, "I give the book to her" becomes [IT:Le do il libro].

[PAUSE 4s]

### Forms of Indirect Object Pronouns

Here are the forms of Italian indirect object pronouns:

[IT:mi] – to me
[IT:ti] – to you, singular informal
[IT:gli] – to him
[IT:le] – to her
[IT:ci] – to us
[IT:vi] – to you, plural
[IT:gli] – to them

[PAUSE 2s]

Notice that [IT:gli] can mean both "to him" and "to them," but the context usually makes it clear which is intended.

[PAUSE 4s]

### NO Agreement in Passato Prossimo

A crucial difference to remember is that unlike direct object pronouns, indirect object pronouns do not require agreement with the past participle in passato prossimo. This means you do not change the past participle to agree with the indirect object pronoun.

[PAUSE 3s]

For example, in [IT:Le ho parlato] - I spoke to her - the past participle [IT:parlato] does not change. No agreement needed.

[PAUSE 4s]

### Position of Indirect Object Pronouns

## SECTION 3: Reflexive Pronouns

Next up, let's talk about reflexive pronouns in Italian. Reflexive pronouns are an essential part of the Italian language, as they allow us to express actions where the subject and object are the same. This means the action reflects back on the subject.

[PAUSE 3s]

In Italian, reflexive pronouns are used when the subject of a verb is also the object. The action is done and received by the same person. Here are the forms of reflexive pronouns in Italian:

[IT:mi] for "myself"
[IT:ti] for "yourself", singular
[IT:si] for "himself", "herself", "itself", or "themselves"
[IT:ci] for "ourselves"
[IT:vi] for "yourselves", plural
[IT:si] again for "themselves" or "each other" in reciprocal actions

[PAUSE 3s]

Let's look at some examples to illustrate these pronouns in action:

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT:Mi lavo] - I wash myself.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Ti vesti] - You dress yourself.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Si sveglia] - He or she wakes up.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Ci prepariamo] - We prepare ourselves.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Vi alzate] - You all get up.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Si divertono] - They enjoy themselves.

[PAUSE 4s]

Now, it's important to note that in the past tense, specifically the passato prossimo, Italian reflexive verbs always use the auxiliary verb [IT:essere]. This is crucial because it affects how the past participle agrees with the subject in gender and number.

[PAUSE 3s]

For example:

[IT:Mi sono lavato] - I washed myself, when the speaker is masculine.

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT:Mi sono lavata] - I washed myself, when the speaker is feminine.

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT:Si sono visti] - They saw each other.

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT:Ci siamo divertiti] - We enjoyed ourselves, when it's a masculine group or a mixed group.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Si è alzata] - She got up.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Ti sei lavato] - You washed yourself, when you're masculine.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Vi siete svegliati] - You all woke up, when it's masculine or a mixed group.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Si sono incontrati] - They met each other.

[PAUSE 4s]

There are three main types of reflexive verbs in Italian: true reflexive, reciprocal, and pronominal.

[PAUSE 2s]

**True Reflexive**: This is when the subject performs an action on itself. For example, [IT:Mi lavo] - I wash myself.

[PAUSE 2s]

**Reciprocal**: This is when two or more subjects perform an action on each other. For example, [IT:Si sono visti] - They saw each other.

[PAUSE 2s]

**Pronominal**: These verbs include reflexive pronouns but don't have a direct translation into English. They often express actions that concern the subject in a particular way. For instance, [IT:Me ne vado] - I'm leaving - where [IT:andarsene] is a pronominal verb.

[PAUSE 4s]

Common reflexive verbs include [IT:svegliarsi] - to wake up, [IT:alzarsi] - to get up, and [IT:lavarsi] - to wash oneself. These verbs can greatly enrich your Italian vocabulary as they are used frequently in daily conversation.

One: Reflexive pronouns are used when the subject and object of the verb are the same.

[PAUSE 2s]

Two: The forms are [IT:mi], [IT:ti], [IT:si], [IT:ci], [IT:vi], and [IT:si].

[PAUSE 2s]

## SECTION 4: NE Pronoun

Moving on to our next topic, let's explore the fascinating world of the NE pronoun. This tiny yet mighty word plays a crucial role in Italian, serving two main purposes: indicating quantity and referring to a topic.

[PAUSE 3s]

Firstly, let's talk about NE's function in terms of quantity. NE is often used to express 'of it' or 'of them' in English. It allows speakers to avoid repetition and provide clarity without overloading sentences.

[PAUSE 3s]

For example:

[IT: Ne voglio due] - I want two of them.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Ne ho tre] - I have three of them.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Ne prendo un po'] - I take some of it.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Ne ho comprati tre] - I bought three of them, when they're masculine.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Ne ho comprate due] - I bought two of them, when they're feminine.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Ne bevo molto] - I drink a lot of it.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Ne hai?] - Do you have any?

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Vuoi comprarne?] - Do you want to buy some?

[PAUSE 3s]

In these examples, NE replaces the partitive phrase and precedes the verb. Notice how the verb sometimes changes to agree with the quantity, especially when specific amounts are involved, as in examples four and five. This agreement is optional but can provide additional clarity.

[PAUSE 4s]

Secondly, NE is used to refer to a topic or a subject that has been previously mentioned, often translated as 'about it' in English.

[PAUSE 2s]

Here are some examples:

[IT: Ne parliamo] - We talk about it.

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT: Gliene parlo] - I talk to him or her about it.

[PAUSE 3s]

In these sentences, NE represents an idea or a subject that is already known in the conversation, eliminating the need to repeat information.

[PAUSE 4s]

Now, let's dive into the positioning of NE. Typically, NE is placed before the conjugated verb. However, when used with infinitives, imperatives, or gerunds, it can be attached to the end. This flexibility allows for fluidity in sentence construction:

[PAUSE 2s]

[IT: Parlane con lui] - Talk about it with him.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: È importante parlarne] - It's important to talk about it.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Stiamo parlandone] - We are talking about it.

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Ne parlerai domani?] - Will you talk about it tomorrow?

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT: Non posso farne a meno] - I can't do without it.

[PAUSE 4s]

To recap, NE serves two primary functions: indicating quantity - meaning 'of it' or 'of them' - and referencing a topic - meaning 'about it'. It typically precedes the verb but can be attached to infinitives, imperatives, and gerunds. While quantity agreement is optional, it can enhance understanding when specific numbers are involved.

[PAUSE 3s]

Remember, mastering NE takes practice. Start by identifying situations where NE can simplify your sentences, and gradually incorporate it into your conversations. With time, using NE will become second nature, enhancing your fluency and making your Italian sound more natural.

## SECTION 5: Combined Pronouns

Building on everything we've learned so far, let's now tackle combined pronouns. Specifically, we will explore what happens when two pronouns meet, how to apply the "E" rule, the special forms for the third person, and the order of pronouns.

[PAUSE 3s]

### When Two Pronouns Meet

In Italian, it's common to use both indirect and direct object pronouns together in a sentence. This can seem a bit daunting at first, but with practice, it becomes second nature. The key is understanding the order and transformation of pronouns.

[PAUSE 4s]

### The "E" Rule: [IT:mi]/[IT:ti]/[IT:ci]/[IT:vi] → [IT:me]/[IT:te]/[IT:ce]/[IT:ve]

When combining pronouns, the indirect object pronouns [IT:mi] - to me, [IT:ti] - to you, [IT:ci] - to us, and [IT:vi] - to you all - change to [IT:me], [IT:te], [IT:ce], and [IT:ve] respectively. This transformation occurs when they are followed by a direct object pronoun or [IT:ne].

[PAUSE 3s]

Here are some examples:

[IT:Me lo dai?] - Do you give it to me?

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Te la compro] - I buy it for you

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Ce li portano] - They bring them to us

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Ve le mando] - I send them to you all

[PAUSE 4s]

### Special [IT:GLIELO] Forms for 3rd Person

When it comes to the third person, things get a bit more specific. The indirect object pronouns [IT:gli] - to him or to them - and [IT:le] - to her - combine with direct object pronouns to form special words.

[PAUSE 3s]

For example:

[IT:Glielo do] - I give it to him, to her, or to them

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Gliela mando] - I send it to him, to her, or to them

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Glieli porto] - I bring them to him, to her, or to them

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Gliele dico] - I tell them to him, to her, or to them

[PAUSE 4s]

### Order: Indirect + Direct or [IT:NE]

The order of pronouns in Italian is quite logical: the indirect object pronoun always comes before the direct object pronoun or [IT:ne]. This is a fundamental rule and applies consistently.

[PAUSE 3s]

Here are some examples to illustrate this:

[IT:Me ne dai?] - Do you give me some?

[IT:Te ne parlo] - I talk to you about it

[IT:Gliene ho parlato] - I talked to him or her about it

[IT:Me l'ha dato] - He gave it to me

[PAUSE 1s]

[IT:Te lo voglio dire] - I want to tell it to you

Now, let's add a few more examples to solidify your understanding:
